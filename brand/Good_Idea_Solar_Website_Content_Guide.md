# Good Idea Solar — Website Content Guide

*Version 2.0 · Page-by-page build guide for the MVP website. Pair this with the Brand Guide and Brand Cheat Sheet. Copy in `code blocks` and block quotes is launch-ready; use it as written.*

---

## Project Objective

Build a professional, stable website that serves as a credibility check for potential partners, investors, farmers, and community stakeholders. This is **not** a lead-generation machine. The primary purpose is to answer: *"Is Good Idea Solar a legitimate company that I want to work with?"*

Secondary goals: direct visitors to social media, allow interested farmers/landowners to contact us, showcase current projects, and demonstrate experience and values.

Most important pages: **About Us** and **Projects**.

---

## Site Architecture

**Navigation:** Home · About Us · Projects · Contact

About Us absorbs "How It Works" for now. Five pages is the maximum at this stage — the biggest risk for an early-stage company website is trying to look bigger than you are.

*Optional future pages (v2):* News & Updates, Community Solar Resources, Investor Relations.

## PROJECTS PAGE

Purpose: **build trust and prove execution** ("Can these people actually develop solar projects?"). Likely the highest-intent page.

### Project Card / Detail structure
1. **Project Hero** — name, location, status, hero image
2. **Project Snapshot** — capacity, partner farmland, solar footprint, status
3. **About the Project** — one-paragraph description
4. **Farm Impact** — Partner Farmland · Solar Footprint · Remaining Agricultural Land · Agricultural Use · Farm Status
5. **Community Impact** — local energy generation, community benefits
6. **Development Timeline** — current phase (e.g., Site Identified → Feasibility Complete → Interconnection Submitted → Development Underway)
7. **Photo Gallery** — farm, construction, project, drone
8. **Contact CTA** — "Interested in exploring whether solar is a good fit for your property? Discuss Your Property →"

### The Four Launch Projects (final descriptions)

**Henderson County Sheriff's Office** — Lexington, Tennessee · 800 kW · 5 Acres Partner Farmland · 3 Acres Solar · Pasture · In Development
> Developed in partnership with Henderson County, Tennessee, this project utilizes unused land reserved for county EMS communications infrastructure. The project demonstrates how underutilized land can generate local renewable energy while supporting public services and community resilience.

**Libertytown Sheep Farm** — Berlin, Maryland · 3.96 MW · 81 Acres Partner Farmland · 20 Acres Solar · Sheep · In Development
> The Libertytown Sheep Farm project supports the expansion of a local sheep farming operation onto a second property. The solar installation is being designed to allow continued grazing, with the panels serving as shade structures for livestock while creating a long-term source of farm income.
> *Suggested images: `JSG_Sheep1.jpeg`, `JSG_Sheep2.jpeg`, `Sheep3.jpg`.*

**Longridge Road** — Parsonsburg, Maryland · 4.17 MW · 102 Acres Partner Farmland · 14 Acres Solar · Agricultural Land (corn/soybeans; designed for future hay) · In Development
> The Longridge Road project is being developed in collaboration with local farmers and agricultural stakeholders to demonstrate how solar development and active farming can successfully coexist. The property is currently used for corn and soybean production and is being evaluated for future hay production alongside solar development.
> *Suggested images: `JSG_Crops1.jpeg`, `Rutgers_Soy.jpeg`.*

**Sandy Spring CSA** — Ashton, Maryland · 2.0 MW · 20 Acres Partner Farmland · 7 Acres Solar · Table Crops · In Development
> The Sandy Spring CSA project is designed as a regional agrivoltaics demonstration site exploring how food production can continue alongside solar generation. The farm currently grows produce for local food assistance programs and will help evaluate which table crops perform best under and around solar arrays.
> *Suggested images: `Rutgers_Peppers.jpeg`, `JSG_Crops2.jpeg`.*

**What each demonstrates:** Henderson County → community & public-sector partnership · Libertytown → livestock agrivoltaics · Longridge Road → farm-community collaboration · Sandy Spring CSA → agricultural innovation & food production.

*(Project data and a sortable table are in `Good_Idea_Solar_Project_Data.xlsx`.)*

## Design Guidelines

- **Tone:** simple, professional, trustworthy, farmer-focused.
- **Avoid:** corporate buzzwords, heavy technical language, excessive animations.
- **Preferred images:** real farms, real projects, real communities, real team members — use the `/Images` assets first.
- **Avoid images:** generic stock photos, urban imagery, overly futuristic solar imagery.
- **Colors:** ~60% White · 25% Blue (#143B90) · 10% Green (#007562) · 5% Yellow (#FFF200). Yellow is accent/fill only — never yellow text on white.
- **Fonts:** Poppins (headings), Source Sans Pro (body) — both free on Google Fonts.

## Technical Requirements

Mobile responsive · fast loading · SSL enabled · Google Analytics · linked social media accounts (LinkedIn at minimum) · contact-form submission notifications to info@goodideasolar.com · SEO basics configured (page titles, meta descriptions, favicon = meatball icon) · 99%+ uptime.

## Success Criteria

A visitor should leave the website understanding:
1. Who Good Idea Solar is
2. Why the company exists
3. How it helps farms
4. Why it is different from traditional developers
5. How to get in touch

If those five questions are answered clearly, the website is successful.
