## 2. Brand Overview

### Who We Are

Good Idea Solar is a farmer-first solar development company. We help farms create long-term income through solar projects while keeping farmland productive and supporting local communities. Founded from a family farm challenge, Good Idea Solar exists to create opportunities that benefit farms, communities, and project partners alike.

Rather than maximizing our own return at the expense of the landowner, we structure projects to create lasting value for the farm, the family, the community, and our company. We **partner** with farmers — we don't buy or rent their land out from under them — and we share the revenue the solar produces. If we do our jobs correctly, the farm wins, the community wins, and Good Idea Solar succeeds as a result.

### Mission

Help farms remain productive, economically viable, and connected to their communities through responsible solar development.

*Heritage line (from the original deck, still true to who we are): "Good Idea Solar was founded by farmers for farmers. We help families preserve their legacy while giving back to their community and promoting local sustainability."*

### Vision

A future where family farms remain economically viable for generations through innovative partnerships and responsible renewable energy development.

### Brand Promise

We help farmers earn more from their land without sacrificing it. Solar should support the farm — not replace it.

### The Big Idea

> **Solar should be another crop to support the farm.**

This is a worldview, not a marketing line. It ties together the company's origin story, the website headline, the farmer-first philosophy, and the long-term agrivoltaics direction. The deck phrases the same idea as letting the farmer **"harvest the sun"** — a steady, reliable income stream grown right alongside the existing crops. It positions Good Idea Solar as a farm-minded organization that uses solar as a tool — not a solar company that happens to work with farms.

## 3. Our Story

Good Idea Solar was founded on a simple idea: solar should be another crop to support the farm.

Our story began when founder Justin Aydelotte inherited his family's farm and faced a challenge familiar to many landowners: the farm was still actively farming but was operating at a loss. We realized we had to do something different in order to keep the family farm profitable.

As we explored our options, we discovered that using a small portion of the farm for solar could create stable, long-term income while preserving the rest of the land for agriculture. What started as a solution for one family farm became a mission to help others.

Today, Good Idea Solar partners with farmers and landowners to develop solar projects that create new sources of revenue while supporting local communities and expanding access to clean, locally generated energy. We believe successful projects are built on trust, transparency, and long-term partnerships.

Our team brings together expertise in agriculture, engineering, community engagement, finance, and renewable energy development. While our backgrounds are diverse, we share a common goal: helping farms remain productive, profitable, and positioned for the future.

## 4. The Problem & The Solution (the case we make)

These are the substance behind the "Problem" and "Solution" sections of the website. The numbers come from the company's own materials *(source: deck)* and should be presented plainly, never as fear-based marketing.

### The Problem
- It's too expensive to be a small farmer. Between changing weather patterns, volatile crop and commodity prices, yield variability, and rising labor costs, **the average small family farm loses about $4 per acre per year from farming.**
- Yet small family farms own roughly **89% of all farmland in the United States** — they are enormous community assets that are financially squeezed.
- Farmers want an alternative that works — without giving up their land or their way of life.

### The Solution
- Good Idea Solar converts **a portion** of an active farm to solar, keeping most of the farm in agriculture and **sharing the solar revenue with the farmer.**
- Our method can earn a farm **up to $20,000 per acre per year, at no cost to the farmer**, while producing clean, renewable energy at **below-market rates** for the surrounding community.
- We call it letting the farmer "harvest the sun": a new, stable crop grown alongside the existing ones.

> **Messaging caution for the website:** the "$20,000 per acre" and "$4 per acre loss" figures are powerful but financial. On the public site, lead with the *farmer outcome* ("a new, stable source of long-term income, at no cost to you") and use specific dollar figures sparingly and only where they can be substantiated. Detailed revenue numbers, financial projections, and per-project economics stay **off** the public site (see §17).

## 5. Positioning

### Positioning Statement

For farmers and landowners seeking new ways to strengthen their financial future, Good Idea Solar is the development partner that puts farmers first. Unlike traditional solar developers, we work alongside farmers to keep land productive, maximize long-term value, and create solutions that benefit both the farm and the surrounding community.

### What Makes Us Different

| Traditional Solar Developers | Good Idea Solar |
|---|---|
| Focus on acquiring land rights and maximizing project economics | Focus on preserving farms, creating long-term partnerships, and generating sustainable income for farming families |
| Often out-of-state, not local to the community | Local relationships within the communities we serve |
| Treat farmers as vendors / land to acquire | Treat farmers as partners; we succeed when the farm succeeds |
| Offer a flat **lease** | Offer a **revenue share** of the solar profits — meaningfully higher returns for the farmer |
| Buy or rent the land | **Partner** with the farmer and keep the land in agriculture |

### The Competitive Landscape *(source: deck)*

Good Idea Solar is the rare player that is **locally based, farmer-focused, and capable of long-term investment** all at once. Competitors each cover only part of that:

- **Land brokers / real estate agents** — transact land but don't develop or share long-term value.
- **Front-end solar developers** — develop projects but offer farmers a traditional lease and aren't rooted in the community.
- **Long-term asset owners** — hold projects but don't source land or build local relationships.

**How we're different:** We source the land *and* develop the project in-house, giving the landowner clarity and the project higher returns. We offer a **revenue share** rather than a lease — higher returns for the farmer and, for us, an effectively unlimited supply of land. And because farmers refer their neighbors, we get head-of-line privileges on new development opportunities.

### Our Philosophy

If we do our jobs correctly:

- The farm wins.
- The community wins.
- Good Idea Solar succeeds as a result.

### The Misconception We Must Overcome

That "we're just like everyone else." We're not — we are a service-based organization that puts farmers and farms first.

### Why Farmers Choose Us

We offer a lifeline to the farmer. We work with the agriculture to make sure the farm stays in production, and we provide significantly more revenue to the farmer than other solar developers. We're local to the community, not an outside developer parachuting in. Because we partner instead of buying or renting, we also lower our own operating and customer-acquisition costs — and become part of the farmer's community and local network.

## 6. Target Audience

### Primary Audience — Farmers & Landowners

- **Land:** 5–25 acres of developable land *(the founder's ideal-customer target; the broader addressable market in company materials runs to small family farms of roughly 5–100 acres — a segment "often overlooked by larger solar developers," i.e., an untapped market).* 
- **Geography:** States with active community solar programs (currently Tennessee and Maryland).
- **Project economics:** Each project needs to be investible relative to the market for us to develop it.
- **Age:** Not a factor — we work with all interested parties.
- **Biggest concern:** Access to electrical infrastructure.

The audience is intentionally broad and includes anyone trying to keep farmland economically viable: someone who inherited a farm, someone who just bought a farm, a retiring farmer, a first-generation farmer, an absentee landowner with agricultural property, or someone who wants to acquire farmland but needs another revenue stream to make the economics work.

**Customer characteristics:** community-oriented, long-term thinkers, interested in preserving family land, open to new opportunities, seeking stable supplemental income, looking for alternatives to traditional solar leases.

**Common concerns:** losing productive farmland, working with out-of-state developers, lack of trust in the solar industry, complexity of solar projects, access to electrical infrastructure, long-term impact on their property.

### Secondary Audience — Community Stakeholders

Local governments, economic development organizations, utilities, schools, hospitals, and community solar subscribers. *(Several of our projects are anchored by public-sector and community offtakers — e.g., a county sheriff's office / EMS site and a community-supported-agriculture farm — which reinforces this audience.)*

## 8. Brand Voice & Tone

Our voice is closest to a **trusted neighbor + business operator + project developer**. We are **not** a corporate consultant, environmental activist, Silicon Valley startup, or utility company. A farmer landing on our site should feel like they're talking to a knowledgeable neighbor who understands development.

### We Are
Practical · Approachable · Honest · Competent · Service-Oriented · Farmer-Focused · Optimistic · Confident without being aggressive.

### We Are Not
Corporate · Technical for technical's sake · Pushy · Political · Sales-driven · Buzzword-heavy.

### Formal vs. Casual
Casual. Plain language over technical jargon.

### What We Are (with example phrasing)
- **Neighbors:** "We'd be happy to walk the property and discuss whether solar is a good fit."
- **Partners:** "We work with farmers to find the right solution."
- **Problem solvers:** "Many farms are looking for new sources of stable income."
- **Experienced:** "We understand both the agricultural and development sides of the project."

### What We Are Not (with corrections)
- Not salespeople — ✗ "Unlock the hidden value of your property today!" → ✓ "Let's see whether solar makes sense for your farm."
- Not corporate — ✗ "Leveraging innovative renewable infrastructure solutions to maximize stakeholder value." → ✓ "We help farms create long-term income through solar development."
- Not activists — ✗ "Join the clean energy revolution." → ✓ "Solar can provide a stable source of income while supporting the local community."

Environmental benefits matter, but they are never the lead message.

### Writing Rules
1. **Use plain English.** ✗ "Distributed generation assets create long-term revenue streams." → ✓ "Solar projects can create stable income for decades."
2. **Lead with the farm.** ✗ "Our solar projects provide renewable electricity." → ✓ "Our projects help farms generate additional income." Electricity comes second.
3. **Explain before educating.** ✗ "Community solar allows subscribers to receive virtual net-metering credits." → ✓ "Community solar lets local residents benefit from nearby solar projects without installing panels on their own property."
4. **Be specific.** ✗ "We create value." → ✓ "We help farms create long-term income."
5. **Talk like a human.** ✗ "Stakeholder engagement is central to our process." → ✓ "We spend time getting to know the communities where we work."

### Language: Preferred vs. Avoid
- **Preferred words:** Partner, Partnership, Farm, Family, Community, Legacy, Stewardship, Opportunity, Long-term, Local, Harvest.
- **Avoid excessive use of:** Asset, Stakeholder, Platform, Ecosystem, Synergy, Transformation, Optimization, Disruption.

## 9. Messaging

### Message Hierarchy

Always communicate in this order. Solar is the solution; the farm is the priority.

1. **Productive Farmland**
2. **Long-Term Income**
3. **Community Benefit**
4. **Renewable Energy**

Most solar companies lead with #4. Good Idea Solar leads with #1 and #2.

*(Origin note: the earliest hierarchy was Farm Preservation → Farmer Income → Community Benefit → Renewable Energy. It was refined toward the constructive framing above — "create productive, profitable farmland" rather than "save the farm" — to leave room for the company's longer-term vision of helping people acquire and keep farmland.)*

### Key Messaging Pillars
- Preserve / sustain productive farmland
- Build stronger communities
- Create sustainable, long-term income
- Farmer-first partnerships
- Responsible development

### Hero (Approved)

> **Solar for the Farm, Not the Farm for Solar**
>
> Good Idea Solar partners with family farms to create long-term income through solar development while preserving productive farmland and strengthening local communities.
>
> [View Our Projects] · [Contact Us]

Why it works: "Partners with" reinforces service orientation; "long-term income" is the farmer benefit; "preserving productive farmland" is the differentiator; "strengthening local communities" expands the impact; no unnecessary solar-industry jargon. The hero answers the only question a farmer cares about first: *Why should I care?*

### Trust Bar (immediately below hero)

**Built by Farmers for Farmers**
✓ Family Farm Roots ✓ Community-Focused Development ✓ Long-Term Partnerships ✓ Renewable Energy Expertise

### Taglines (short, for use throughout the site)
- Built by Farmers for Farmers

*(Note: "Solar for the Farm, Not the Farm for Solar" is the headline / rallying cry — it is intentionally too long to serve as the short tagline.)*

**Good:** Solar for the Farm, Not the Farm for Solar · Built by Farmers for Farmers · Helping Farms Create Long-Term Income · A Different Approach to Solar Development · Keeping Land Productive for Future Generations.

**Avoid:** Revolutionizing Renewable Infrastructure · Unlocking Landowner Value · Accelerating the Energy Transition · Innovative Clean Energy Solutions · Maximizing Stakeholder Outcomes.

## 11. Visual Identity

### The Logo

The Good Idea Solar logo is a circular emblem ("meatball") set beside a stacked, all-caps wordmark. The emblem reads as a single scene that captures all three parts of the business:

- a **yellow sun** with radiating arc lines (solar energy / optimism),
- a **green hillside** (agriculture, growth),
- and a **blue field rendered as a solar-panel grid** with yellow gridlines (project development / infrastructure),
- all held by a confident **black outline**, with the wordmark **"GOOD IDEA / SOLAR"** in a bold, geometric, all-caps sans (consistent with the Poppins heading choice).

This is efficient branding: most solar logos communicate only "solar." Ours communicates sun + farm + project at a glance.

**Color values confirmed by sampling the master logo art** (they match the approved palette, with one note):
- Sun yellow `#FFF200` · Panel blue `#143B90` · Field green `#007562` · Outline & wordmark **pure black `#000000`**.
- Note: the logo's outline and wordmark are pure black `#000000`. Use pure black for the logo and logo-adjacent marks; use Charcoal `#1D1F1F` for long-form body text (slightly softer for reading).

### Logo Usage

**Primary logo (horizontal):** default for website header, footer, documents, presentations, letterhead. → `final-01.jpg` (root) or `Branding/final-01.png` (transparent raster), `final.ai` / `final.eps` / `final.pdf` (vector).

**Secondary logo (circular "meatball"):** use when space is limited — mobile navigation, social media profile images, favicons, watermarks. → `Branding/Meatball.jpg`.

**Approved backgrounds:** Preferred — White, Light Gray (the logo was designed for light backgrounds). Acceptable — Dark Navy, Charcoal, but on dark backgrounds use the icon only or a reversed wordmark, because the black wordmark disappears on dark.

**Clear space:** minimum padding equal to the width of the "O" in GOOD around the entire logo. No text or graphics inside that area.

**Minimum size:** Horizontal logo — 200px min width (desktop), 140px min width (mobile). Meatball — 32px minimum digital size (smaller loses detail in the panel grid).

**Do:** use approved colors · maintain proportions · use the vector files (`.ai` / `.eps` / `.pdf` / SVG) wherever possible · use transparent PNG/SVG online.

**Don't:** stretch · rotate · change colors · add shadows · place on busy photography · crop the icon.

**Future (not MVP):** export an SVG, and create single-color all-white and all-black versions for embroidery, construction signage, permit documents, watermarks, and merchandise. *(Note: copyright/ownership documentation for the brand is on file at `Branding/Good Idea Solar COPYRIGHTS.pdf`.)*

## 12. Imagery Standards

Guiding rule: **People → Farms → Community → Solar** (not Solar → Technology → Infrastructure). The brand sells **competence**: a farmer must believe both that we understand farms *and* that we can actually deliver a solar project.

### Principles
1. **Real beats perfect.** Use actual Good Idea Solar projects, farms, team members, community events, and landowners (with permission). Avoid generic stock farmers, perfectly manicured corporate photography, AI-generated people, and generic desert solar farms.
2. **Show working land.** Crops, fields, tractors, equipment, livestock, active agricultural operations — this visually proves "Solar for the Farm, Not the Farm for Solar."
3. **Show the outcome, not just the asset.** Strong: a farmer on productive land with solar in the background; a community benefiting from local power; a completed project integrated into the farm. Weak: close-up panels, inverter cabinets, construction equipment. The solar project is *evidence* of success, not the story itself.

### Overall Imagery Mix (balanced)
- **35% Agriculture** — farms, fields, equipment, rural landscapes
- **25% People** — farmers, team, community interactions, site visits
- **30% Projects & Infrastructure** — solar arrays, construction progress, site layouts, drone photography, electrical infrastructure
- **10% Community Impact** — schools, businesses, municipal partners, community events

### Page-Specific Guidance
- **Home** (agriculture-heavy, ~50% farms / 30% people / 20% solar) — answers "Why do these people exist?"
- **About** (people-heavy: team, farm, founder, site visits) — answers "Why should I trust these people?"
- **Projects** (infrastructure-heavy: site maps, drone imagery, installations, construction, timelines) — answers "Can these people actually execute?"
- **Contact** — simple and personal: a team photo or farm landscape; no stock photography.

## 13. Calls to Action

CTAs should feel like the first step in a conversation, not a sales funnel. The site is a credibility check and relationship builder.

### Principles
- **Low pressure** — Good: Contact Us, Start the Conversation, Talk With Our Team, Learn About Your Options. Avoid: Get Started Today, Claim Your Savings, Unlock Revenue Now.
- **Partnership-oriented** — Good: Explore Whether Solar Is Right for Your Farm, Talk With Our Team, Discuss Your Property. Avoid: Submit Your Property, Apply Now, Get Approved.
- **On-brand** — every CTA reinforces Service → Trust → Partnership.

### CTA Hierarchy
| Tier | CTA | Where |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **View Our Projects** | Homepage hero primary |
| 2 | **Contact Us** | Homepage hero secondary; global |
| 3 | **Talk With Our Team** | About page |
| 4 | **Discuss Your Property** | End of every project page |

- **Contact page** — Headline "Let's Start the Conversation." Button: **Send Message** (not Submit / Apply / Request Proposal).
- **Future CTA** (when SEO/projects mature): "See If Your Farm Qualifies" — creates curiosity, not pushy, acknowledges not every property works. Not the primary CTA today.

Key insight: the **Projects page** is likely the real conversion page. End every project page with: *"Interested in exploring whether solar is a good fit for your property? Discuss Your Property →"*
